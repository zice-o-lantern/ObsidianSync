# Why

I race down the stairs, as if I’m afraid of being caught by the miasma of this house. The deafening sound of wood splintering under the soles of my shoes echoes through the house. I tell myself that maybe if I get as far away as possible, I can pretend nothing happened. After all, nothing happened. Just a blunder, nothing more, nothing less. It happens. I didn’t bother him. He told me so. He’s not the one I’m worried about. I freeze at the bottom of the stairs. I’m panting. I catch my breath. I wipe a bead of sweat from my forehead. I take my hand off the banister. My arm is shaking. Those tremors again…
I don’t know why I have this. My muscles can’t handle even slightly intense physical activity. The move was a nightmare. I was lifting two boxes and shaking like a jackhammer. Normally, it calms down after a while. However, right now, I don’t have time. I have to clean. The task will have to wait. I’ll just go to the laundromat tomorrow. I go back to the closet. I grab the rag and start dusting the old dark oak furniture.

Eventually, our efforts continue at a steady pace, and the tremors finally subside, even though the effects of fatigue are still being felt. The living room, which until then had made me sneeze at the slightest nod of my head, almost makes me want to grab a teapot and pour a nice cup of tea on the coffee table, just like my mom used to do when she was still in her right mind. Every time I visited her when I was little, I’d always see her sitting on those brown leather chairs, with dents and nicks here and there. A few cushions were missing buttons, and the imprints of various backsides had left indelible proof of its quality and comfort. You often find yourself dozing off in them. The smell of leather clung to your fingers and gave a hint of something green and wild. The armrests showed their age, all dented from the kids sitting on those intriguing frames—even though they were told repeatedly that it wasn’t a chair. So I didn’t go near it much when Grandma was drinking. Not many people did, really.
Grandma was a recluse. One might think that with her skill at making tea, she’d invite several of her friends over on a sunny Sunday afternoon (which can be rare here, I’ll give you that) to enjoy the fresh air of the English Channel. She’d replied to me on the subject: “No, not at all. They’re all either on the other side of the world, idiots, or dead.”
I didn’t question her. When you get old, you either get lonely or become an idiot—it’s as simple as that.
Besides, she preferred that we play in the garden. “It’s more convenient for Éric to take your picture.” My uncle added, “There are more spots to take nice photos outside.”
